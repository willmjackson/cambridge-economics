Twenty-three of Cambridge's standard undergraduate colleges admit candidates for Economics (L100). Of last year's 1,508 applicants to these colleges, roughly one in nine received an offer. The competition is fierce — but it is not uniform. Some colleges receive twice the applicants per place of their neighbours; some weigh the TMUA harder; some lean heavily on the winter pool.

What follows is the ledger. Sort the columns as you like, click a college to examine it in detail, and try plugging in a TMUA score to see where you would land among each college's offer-holders. The figures come from two Cambridge FOI releases (Aug & Sept 2025) and the University's published admissions statistics for the 2024 cycle. The mature colleges (Hughes Hall, Lucy Cavendish, St Edmund's) and women-only Murray Edwards are not shown — their admissions profiles differ enough that side-by-side comparison is misleading.

The trend, in a sentence. Home applications to the standard Economics colleges climbed roughly a third in three years (612 → 805), while UK offers grew far more slowly (91 → 103). The result: a Home applicant's chances slipped from 14.9% in 2023 to 12.8% in 2025.

The growth is unevenly distributed. St John's, King's, Homerton, Trinity Hall and Clare have all seen Home applications grow by half or more in three years; Pembroke, Sidney Sussex and Magdalene have fallen. The other thing worth noticing is the winter pool: at Girton and Homerton in particular, a large share of recent UK offers were made through the pool rather than direct — meaning these colleges effectively recruit from candidates first allocated elsewhere.
