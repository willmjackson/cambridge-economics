Clare College's admissions guidance is direct: watch the public lectures hosted by the Faculty of Economics.

A great place to start is by watching public lectures by influential economists. The Marshall, Keynes, Meade, and Stone lectures hosted at Cambridge offer not only historical perspective but also a glimpse into how economic thinking continues to evolve. Many of these are available online. — Clare College, advice to Economics applicants

What follows is a catalogue of those four series. Almost every lecture from the last decade has been filmed and posted to the Faculty's YouTube channel. Click any thumbnail to play the lecture in place — no need to leave the page. Each section also has a link to the Faculty's official archive, where lectures from earlier than this catalogue can be found.

A practical note: the Marshall and Keynes lectures are pitched at a general audience and need no prior university-level economics. The Stone and Meade lectures lean closer to research talks. If you've not watched any before, start with a Marshall — Piketty (2017) or Chetty (2015) are excellent introductions.

The Marshall Lectures

Established 1932 · Annual · Two consecutive evenings

The Faculty's flagship annual series, in memory of Alfred Marshall — founder of Cambridge economics and the man who put supply and demand curves into textbooks. Each lecturer gives two consecutive evenings, often with a Q&A. The line-up reads like a roll call of recent Nobel laureates and front-rank economists.

Official archive → YouTube playlist →
Watch first If you watch only one, make it Raj Chetty (2015) on equality of opportunity, or Thomas Piketty (2017) on inequality and capital — both accessible, both consequential.

The Stone Lectures

Established 2009 · Annual · Single evening

Honouring Sir Richard Stone (1913–1991), the Cambridge economist whose work on national income accounting won him the 1984 Nobel Prize. The lectures tend toward applied empirical work — labour, development, education, firms, AI — drawing speakers who often present a recent paper rather than a sweeping retrospective.

Official archive →
Watch first David Autor (2025) on AI and the future of expertise is fresh and topical; Angus Deaton (2010) on measuring global development is a beautifully clear introduction to development economics.

The Keynes Lectures

Established 2018 · Annual · Single evening

Cambridge's newest of the four series, run jointly with the Keynes Fund and inaugurated in 2018 by Jean Tirole. Each lecturer is paired with a shorter companion interview, which is a friendlier way in if a 90-minute talk feels daunting. Note: the British Academy also runs a "Keynes Lecture in Economics" — a separate London-based series — see the footnote below.

Official archive → YouTube playlist →
Watch first Jean Tirole (2018) on the moral limits of markets, the inaugural lecture — or pair Hélène Rey (2022)'s "Is this Time Different?" with her short interview for a quick crash course in financial crises.

The Meade Lectures

Established 2017 · Biennial · Held at Christ's College

Jointly established by Christ's College and the Faculty of Economics in 2017, honouring James Meade (1907–1995, Nobel 1977). The smallest of the four series — biennial in practice, with only four lectures so far. Tone is closer to a research lecture than a public talk, but each has been a major statement by a distinguished economist.

Official archive →
Watch first Dani Rodrik's (2017) inaugural on whether the era of growth miracles is over — a clear, opinionated tour of decades of development economics in 60 minutes.

Source

All four series are hosted by the Cambridge Faculty of Economics. Videos live on the Faculty's official YouTube channel; lecture detail pages live under econ.cam.ac.uk/lectures/. The list above was verified on 12 May 2026.

A different Keynes

The British Academy Keynes Lecture in Economics is a separate national series, held in London since 1971. Cambridge alumni (Angus Deaton, James Mirrlees, Mervyn King) have given it. Worth knowing about, but distinct from the Cambridge series above.
